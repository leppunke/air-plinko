# CASCADE CHAOS

## The Pitch

Every plinko game asks the same question: *where will the ball go?* CASCADE CHAOS asks a different one: **where do you WANT fifty balls to go — and can you engineer it?**

Two players. One shared peg board. Each player gets 30 seconds and a handful of magnetic deflector shields to place anywhere on their half of the field. Then a trapdoor at the top opens and dumps 50+ colorful balls simultaneously. The avalanche cascades through the peg field, bouncing off player-placed deflectors, splitting and merging in chaotic streams. Balls that land in your scoring bins count. Balls that land in your opponent's bins count for them.

It's tower defense meets plinko. Strategy phase, then spectacle phase. Every round plays different because every deflector layout is different.

## How It Plays

### Phase 1: The Build (30 seconds per player)

Each player receives 8 magnetic deflector shields — small angled wedges with magnets on the back that snap onto the steel peg board. Players place them anywhere on their half of the field, angling them to funnel balls toward their scoring bins and away from their opponent's.

A volunteer runs a visible 30-second countdown timer. Strategy under pressure. Kindergartners slap them on randomly (still works — chaos is the point). Fifth graders agonize over angles. Both have a blast.

### Phase 2: The Avalanche (15 seconds of pure spectacle)

Both players step back. A volunteer pulls a rope that opens the hopper gate at the top. 50+ color-coded balls (25 red, 25 blue, plus 5 gold bonus balls) cascade down simultaneously. The sound alone — a roaring clatter of plastic on wood and steel — draws every head in the gym.

Balls split around deflectors, pile up behind blockers, ricochet off pegs, collide with each other mid-air. Some deflector placements create funnels. Some create dams that burst. Some accidentally send balls straight into the opponent's bins. The crowd goes wild.

### Phase 3: The Count (30 seconds)

Scoring bins at the bottom catch balls:
- **Your color in your bins:** 1 point each
- **Gold balls in your bins:** 5 points each
- **Your color in opponent's bins:** 1 point for THEM

Volunteer counts bins. Takes 20 seconds. Winner declared. Deflectors get pulled off (magnets make reset instant), balls get swept back into the hopper, next two players step up.

**Total cycle time: ~2 minutes per pair of players.**

## What Makes This Different

1. **Agency before randomness.** Every other plinko variant is "launch and hope." This one gives players real decisions that matter. Where you place deflectors changes the outcome. Kids learn cause-and-effect in real time.

2. **The avalanche moment.** No carnival game releases 50 balls at once. The visual spectacle of a cascade of colored balls slamming through a peg field is genuinely unprecedented at a school carnival. It's the kind of thing kids film on their parents' phones.

3. **Infinite replayability.** Every deflector layout creates different ball physics. Kids who played once will want to play again with a "better strategy." The line stays long because return customers are eager.

4. **Scales across all grades.** Young kids place deflectors randomly and love the avalanche. Older kids develop theories about optimal placement. Both are having the time of their lives.

5. **Instant reset.** Magnetic deflectors snap off in seconds. Balls funnel back into the hopper via gravity. No moving parts to break, no electronics, no compressed air.

## The Vibe

Imagine: two kids standing at a 4-foot-wide board covered in pegs, frantically sticking angled shields onto the surface while a timer counts down. A crowd gathers. The timer hits zero. A volunteer yanks a rope. Fifty balls explode out of a hopper and cascade down in a roaring, clattering, multicolored waterfall. Kids are screaming. Parents are filming. Balls are bouncing off deflectors in patterns nobody predicted. Gold balls are ricocheting wildly. The whole thing takes 90 seconds and the next two kids are already fighting over who goes first.

That's CASCADE CHAOS.
