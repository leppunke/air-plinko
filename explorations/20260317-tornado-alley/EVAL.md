# Tornado Alley — Carnival Game Evaluation

## Scoring Rubric
Each heuristic scored 1–10 (10 = best possible).

---

### Overall Entertainment: 9/10
Physically slamming a rubber membrane to blast air across a miniature town is inherently hilarious and satisfying. The THWUMP sound, the balls scattering chaotically off buildings, the 60-second pressure — this is a sensory overload in the best way. Deducting 1 point because the cannon reload time could feel frustrating for impatient kids.

### Memorability: 9/10
"Remember that game where you shot tornadoes at a tiny town?" — this is a sentence kids will say. The vortex cannon mechanic is unusual enough that most kids will have never experienced anything like it. The miniature town aesthetic adds character. The sound alone (THWUMP THWUMP THWUMP) will echo in memories.

### Ease of Construction: 6/10
The table itself is straightforward — plywood, edge walls, legs. But the storm shelters with one-way flaps, 6 vortex cannons, and 3D-printed buildings add significant complexity. The cannons are easy individually (bucket + rubber band) but building 6 of them and mounting them properly takes time. Two-weekend build is realistic but tight. The 3D printing dependency could be a bottleneck.

### Appeal by Elementary School Grade
| Grade | Appeal (1-10) | Notes |
|-------|--------------|-------|
| K–1st | 7/10 | Love the chaos and sound. May struggle with cannon strength (small hands). Could use smaller 3-gallon buckets for littles |
| 2nd–3rd | 10/10 | Sweet spot. Strong enough to fire, wild enough to love the chaos, competitive enough to care about score |
| 4th–5th | 9/10 | Strategic play emerges — choosing ports, timing blasts. Might feel slightly "young" for the coolest 5th graders but the destruction keeps them hooked |

### Skill to Luck Spectrum: 7/10 (Great mix)
**Skill factors:** Port selection matters — angling through P1-A vs P1-C sends air to different parts of the field. Timing the reload rhythm. Reading where balls cluster.
**Luck factors:** Ball-on-ball collisions are unpredictable. Building ricochets are chaos. The vortex disperses in semi-random patterns.
This is exactly the right balance — skilled players win more often, but any kid can get a lucky THWUMP that sends 3 balls into the 100-pt shelter.

### Chaos Level: 9/10
Six balls flying around simultaneously, bouncing off buildings, air blasts colliding mid-field. This is barely controlled pandemonium. The only thing keeping it from 10 is the one-way flaps — once a ball scores, it's locked in, which gradually reduces the chaos. That said, the final 15 seconds with 2 balls left and both players frantically blasting is peak carnival energy.

### Durability: 7/10
**Strong points:** Plywood table is bombproof. Buckets are cheap to replace. Whiffle balls are disposable.
**Weak points:** Rubber membranes will fatigue after hundreds of pulls — keep spare exercise bands. One-way flaps will need periodic replacement. 3D-printed buildings could break if a kid reaches in and grabs one (but the acrylic lid prevents this during play). Acrylic lid is shatter-resistant but could scratch over time.
**Repairability:** All fragile parts are cheap and quick to swap. Membranes take 2 minutes to replace. Buildings can be reprinted overnight.

### Photogenicity: 8/10
The miniature town layout is Instagram-ready even without players. During gameplay, the action is mostly horizontal and visible through the clear lid — phones can capture the chaos from above or from the side cross-section view. The red/blue player color coding photographs well. Deducting points because the vortex itself is invisible (unless you add fog, which bumps this to 10/10).

### Onboarding Time: 8/10 (Very fast — closer to cornhole)
**30-second explanation:** "Blast the balls into the shelters with the tornado cannon. Pull, aim at a hole, release. 60 seconds. Go."
Kids understand "shoot air at balls" immediately. The scoring is painted on the shelters. The only learning curve is the reload rhythm (pull-release), which takes 2-3 tries to get. No rules ambiguity — ball is in the shelter or it isn't.

## Verdict
Tornado Alley trades some construction simplicity for an absolutely unhinged gameplay experience. The vortex cannon mechanic is a genuine differentiator — no kid has played this before. The miniature town adds character and narrative ("save the town!"). The 60-second timer creates urgency. This is the game that gets a line wrapped around the booth.

**Biggest risk:** The vortex cannons need to reliably move whiffle balls across 3+ feet. Testing the air physics early is critical — if the cannons feel wimpy, the whole game falls flat. Prototype one cannon + one ball + the table gap before committing to the full build.
