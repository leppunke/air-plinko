# Tornado Alley — Carnival Game Evaluation

## Scoring Rubric
Each heuristic scored 1–10 (10 = best possible).

---

### Overall Entertainment: 6/10
The core loop — pull membrane, aim at port, release — has a satisfying physicality to it. The THWUMP sound is genuinely fun the first few times. But the reload cycle is a real problem: after each blast, the player has to pull the membrane back, reposition to a port, and fire again. That's 3-4 seconds of downtime per shot in a 60-second game, meaning a kid gets maybe 12-15 shots total. Compare that to a game where you're continuously doing something (throwing balls, launching with a slingshot). A third of your play time is spent resetting. For younger kids who fumble the membrane pull, it's worse — they could spend more time reloading than playing. The fun is real but it's intermittent, not continuous.

### Memorability: 6/10
The vortex cannon concept is unusual, and kids probably haven't seen one at a carnival before. But "unusual" and "memorable" aren't the same thing. A kid describing this to a friend would likely say "I hit air at balls and tried to push them into holes" — which sounds a lot like a less exciting version of games where you actually throw or launch something. The miniature town aesthetic is a nice touch, but kids won't remember set dressing. They remember what they did with their hands and whether they won. The THWUMP sound is satisfying in person but doesn't translate to a story you tell afterward. The vortex itself is invisible — you can't see the thing that makes this concept supposedly special.

### Ease of Construction: 5/10
The table is simple plywood, but that's maybe 20% of this build. The real work: 6 vortex cannons that need consistent air output (bucket diameter, membrane tension, and port hole sizing all affect performance and need to be dialed in through trial and error), storm shelters with functioning one-way flaps (mechanical parts that need to work reliably hundreds of times), 3D-printed buildings (requires access to a printer, hours of print time, and reprints when the first versions don't fit or break), and an acrylic lid cut and mounted to the whole assembly. A parent volunteer with a drill and a free weekend cannot build this. You need someone with prototyping experience who can troubleshoot the cannon physics when the first version doesn't move a ball far enough. The 3D printing alone is a hard dependency that most school communities can't easily access.

### Skill to Luck Spectrum: 5/10 (Luck-heavy, possibly frustrating)
**Skill factors:** Port selection gives some directional control. A practiced player might develop a reload rhythm.
**Luck factors:** Vortex rings disperse unpredictably over distance. Ball-on-ball collisions are random. The air puff's effect on a whiffle ball depends on exactly where it hits, the ball's current spin, and whether it's resting against a building. A skilled player aiming carefully may see the same result as a kid randomly blasting — because the air physics just aren't precise enough to reward aim consistently.
The problem isn't the skill/luck ratio itself — it's that the connection between player action and ball movement is too loose. In a good carnival game, you feel like your action caused the outcome. Here, you fire air into a box and things may or may not happen. That ambiguity cuts both ways.

### Chaos Level: 5/10
This score depends entirely on whether the vortex cannons can actually move whiffle balls with force. If they can, you get balls bouncing off buildings and each other — that's fun. If the air puffs are weak (which is a real possibility with DIY bucket cannons operated by children), you get six balls sitting mostly still while kids huff air at them through holes. Whiffle balls are 24 grams. A vortex ring from a 5-gallon bucket dissipates significantly over 3+ feet. The "six balls flying around simultaneously" scenario assumes every puff has meaningful kinetic impact, which is the exact thing that hasn't been tested. Scoring this based on the best-case physics rather than the likely physics is how you end up with a disappointing booth.

### Durability: 6/10
**Strong points:** Plywood table is solid. Buckets and whiffle balls are cheap consumables.
**Weak points:** Rubber membranes are the most-used component and they will stretch and lose snap after dozens of pulls — not hundreds. Once a membrane loses tension, that cannon's air output drops and the game gets worse. You need someone checking and replacing membranes throughout the event, not just between events. One-way flaps are mechanical parts that will jam or tear with repeated ball impacts. 3D-printed PLA buildings are brittle and will crack from ball impacts over a full day of play; reprinting takes hours and requires the printer to be available. The acrylic lid adds a failure point — if it warps, cracks, or gets knocked loose, gameplay stops entirely.
**Repairability:** Individual parts are cheap, but the sheer number of things that can degrade (6 membranes, multiple flaps, multiple buildings, lid mounting) means this booth needs more active maintenance than most.

### Photogenicity: 6/10
The miniature town looks good as a static display. But during gameplay, what does a photo actually capture? Kids hunched over buckets, and balls sitting on a table behind an acrylic lid. The vortex is invisible. The ball movement — if it happens — is too fast and small-scale to read in a phone photo. A parent filming from the side sees their kid pulling a rubber band on a bucket. That's the image. The concept photographs like what it is: kids interacting with buckets. The acrylic lid adds glare in most lighting conditions, making overhead shots difficult. Compare this to a game with a visible projectile arc or a big physical impact moment — those photograph themselves.

### Onboarding Time: 7/10 (Simple concept, physical learning curve)
**30-second explanation:** "Pull back the rubber band, point the bucket at a hole, and let go to blast air. Push the balls into the shelters. 60 seconds."
The concept is easy to explain. But there's a gap between understanding and execution. Kids need to learn the membrane pull (how far back, how to grip it), figure out which port to aim at, and develop a rhythm — all within a 60-second round. The first 15-20 seconds of every game will be fumbling. That's a quarter of the play time lost to onboarding that doesn't happen with simpler mechanics (throw a ball, pull a lever). The parent volunteer also needs to understand the cannon mechanics well enough to help struggling kids, which is a higher ask than most booths.

## Verdict
Do not build this without a working prototype of the core mechanic first — and be prepared for the prototype to fail.

The entire concept rests on whether a DIY bucket vortex cannon, operated by an elementary schooler, can reliably push a whiffle ball 3+ feet across a plywood surface. That is not a known quantity. Vortex cannons are well-documented as a science demo for blowing out candles or pushing fog rings across a room. Using them as a gameplay input to move physical objects with precision and consistency is a different problem. The air disperses. The balls are light and unpredictable. A child's pull strength varies wildly.

If the cannons work — if a 2nd grader can consistently send a ball rolling 3 feet with a single puff — then this concept has a fun core mechanic with a novel feel. But even then, the reload downtime, the 3D printing dependency, the membrane fatigue, and the high active-maintenance burden during an event make this a high-effort booth for uncertain payoff.

**Recommendation:** Build a single cannon and test it against a whiffle ball on a flat surface before investing any further time. If the ball doesn't move convincingly, walk away. If it does, this concept moves to "possible but demanding" — not "must build." There are simpler concepts in this repo that deliver more reliable fun with less construction risk.
