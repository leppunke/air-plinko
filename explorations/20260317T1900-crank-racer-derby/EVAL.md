# THE GREAT CRANK DERBY — Carnival Game Evaluation

## Scoring (1-10 scale)

### Overall Entertainment: 9/10
Pure head-to-head racing with physical exertion. Every game has a winner, a loser, and a crowd that watched it happen. The 30-second game time means constant action and quick turnover. Kids will sprint back to the end of the line.

### Memorability: 9/10
This is the game where "I BEAT TOMMY AND THE BELL RANG AND EVERYONE CHEERED." The physical effort (arms burning on Dead Man's Hill), the audible climax (bell + flag), and the head-to-head format create stories kids retell. Way more memorable than watching a ball bounce through pegs.

### Ease of Construction: 7/10
Moderate complexity. The board itself is simple (plywood + routed grooves). The crank mechanism with ratchet/spool requires 3D-printed parts and some mechanical know-how. Pulley routing needs patience. Two weekends is realistic for two handy dads, but the ratchet mechanism is the trickiest part. No electrical/blower systems needed — huge simplification over air-based games.

### Appeal by Elementary School Grade:
| Grade | Appeal | Notes |
|-------|--------|-------|
| K-1 | 7/10 | May struggle to sustain cranking on Dead Man's Hill. Could offer a "short race" mode (flats only). Still love the bell. |
| 2-3 | 9/10 | Sweet spot. Strong enough to complete the full track, competitive enough to care about winning. |
| 4-5 | 10/10 | Peak demographic. Racing + physical challenge + bragging rights = obsession. Will demand rematches. |

### Skill to Luck Spectrum: 9/10 (Pure Skill)
Zero luck. Zero randomness. The faster and smarter you crank, the more you win. Pacing strategy matters (go hard on flats, conserve on switchbacks, survive Dead Man's Hill, sprint the Glory Run). This is unusual for carnival games — most have heavy luck components. The pure-skill nature means competitive kids will practice and improve, creating a "leaderboard" dynamic naturally.

**Tradeoff note:** Pure skill means younger/weaker kids will consistently lose to older/stronger ones. Consider age-bracket matchmaking or a handicap system (older kids start their car further back).

### Chaos Level: 5/10
Low-chaos compared to plinko variants — no wild bounces, no unpredictable physics. The chaos is SOCIAL: screaming crowds, trash talk, dramatic comebacks on the Glory Run. The game's drama comes from the human element, not from random ball physics. This is a feature, not a bug — but it means the game needs an energetic crowd to really pop.

### Durability: 9/10
Extremely durable. No fragile components:
- Plywood board is bombproof
- Wooden cars sliding on rails don't break
- Nylon cord is cheap and easy to replace (the only consumable)
- 3D-printed crank parts are replaceable in hours
- No acrylic panels to crack, no blower motors to burn out
- Biggest failure mode: string tangling. Mitigated by guide pulleys and proper routing.

### Photogenicity: 8/10
The side-by-side racing visual is great for photos — two kids cranking, cars climbing, crowd watching. The colorful tracks (red/blue) and terrain zone labels add visual interest. The moment of the bell ring + flag pop is a perfect photo op. Less visually spectacular than fog machines and blacklights, but the human drama in the photos makes up for it.

### Onboarding Time: 10/10 (Instant)
"Grab the crank. When I say GO, crank as fast as you can. First car to ring the bell wins."

That's it. Three seconds of instructions. Every kid immediately understands. No rules about scoring zones, no strategy to explain, no "launch then aim" two-step process. Just CRANK. This is as close to cornhole-level simplicity as you can get with a mechanically interesting game.

## Strengths
- Completely different from every other concept — no balls, no pegs, no plinko DNA
- Fastest onboarding of any concept (3-second explanation)
- Highest throughput (30-sec games + 5-sec resets = 100+ kids/hour)
- Most durable design (almost nothing can break)
- Cheapest build ($250-300, no acrylic, no blower)
- The bell. THE BELL. Kids will hear that bell ringing from across the carnival and come running.

## Weaknesses
- Low chaos score — relies on crowd energy rather than mechanical unpredictability
- Pure skill can alienate younger/weaker kids (needs age-matching)
- Visually simpler than sensory-overload concepts (no fog, no lights, no sound effects)
- Less "wow factor" at first glance — the magic is in playing it, not in watching from afar

## The Big Question
Does a school carnival want a GAME or a SPECTACLE? The plinko variants are spectacles — you watch balls bounce through crazy stuff. The Crank Derby is a GAME — you compete, you exert, you win or lose. Both are valid. This one bets on competition being the core memory, not visual chaos.
