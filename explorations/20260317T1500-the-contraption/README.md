# THE CONTRAPTION

## Concept
A massive 4×6 ft vertical game board where every single interaction point is a unique 3D-printed mechanism — not pegs, not bumpers, but honest-to-god *machines*. Spinning wheels, pendulum gates, gear-driven ball lifts, funnel spirals, see-saw catapults, ball-splitters, trap doors, and chain-reaction triggers. Two players launch whiffle balls from the bottom via crank-powered lift mechanisms (also 3D printed). Balls enter a Rube Goldberg wonderland where every run plays out differently because the mechanisms have *state* — a pendulum that swung left for one ball swings right for the next, a see-saw that's loaded tips the opposite way, a gear wheel that's advanced one tooth changes the next ball's path.

Two months. Two Bambu Labs printers. 2,000+ hours of print time. Every single hour shows up on that board.

## The Pitch
*"Crank the handle, launch your ball, and watch it go through THE CONTRAPTION! Spinning wheels! Trap doors! TOTAL CHAOS! Where does your ball end up? Nobody knows — not even the machine!"*

## Gameplay

### Setup
- Giant 4×6 ft board tilted at ~20° from vertical, mounted on a frame peaking at ~7ft
- Two crank stations at the bottom — one per player
- Each crank drives an Archimedes screw lift (3D printed) that carries balls from the collection trough up to the launch zone at the top
- 10 balls per player per round, pre-loaded into the return trough
- Clear acrylic front so the crowd sees EVERYTHING

### The Mechanisms (3D Printed Modules)
Each mechanism mounts to the plywood backboard via a standard bolt pattern (4× M6 bolts on a 4" square). Modules are hot-swappable — break one? Swap in a spare from the print farm. Bored of the layout? Rearrange them between carnival days.

**Tier 1 — Passive Chaos (print time: 1-3 hrs each)**
- **SPINNING WHEELS (×4):** Free-spinning paddle wheels on a bearing. Ball hits a paddle, wheel spins, ball deflects in a random direction. Neon green
- **FUNNEL SPIRALS (×3):** Cone-shaped funnels where the ball spirals around the inside before dropping out the bottom at an unpredictable angle. Translucent purple
- **BUMPER MUSHROOMS (×6):** Oversized mushroom-cap pegs with curved tops that create wild deflections. Classic plinko energy. Hot pink
- **HELIX DROPS (×2):** Vertical helical chutes — ball enters the top, corkscrews down 8", exits at the bottom moving sideways. Mesmerizing to watch. Neon orange

**Tier 2 — Stateful Machines (print time: 4-8 hrs each)**
- **PENDULUM GATES (×3):** A weighted pendulum arm that blocks one of two paths. When a ball pushes through, the pendulum swings to block THAT path and open the other. Every ball alternates direction. Chrome silver
- **SEE-SAW CATAPULTS (×2):** A balanced beam. Ball lands on one side, tips the beam, launches whatever was sitting on the other side. Two balls interact — the first one loads the catapult, the second one triggers it. Bright yellow
- **GEAR RATCHETS (×2):** A ratcheting gear wheel with 6 buckets. Each ball lands in a bucket, advances the wheel one notch, and dumps the PREVIOUS ball out a different exit. Delays and redirects. Metallic blue
- **TRAP DOORS (×3):** Spring-loaded platforms. First ball sits on the door. Second ball hits the trigger pin, door drops, first ball falls through. Timing-dependent chaos. Red

**Tier 3 — Showstopper Centrepieces (print time: 12-20 hrs each)**
- **THE OCTOPUS (×1):** A central hub with 8 articulated arms. Ball enters any arm, gravity and the arm's angle routes it to one of 8 exits. The arms are on loose pivots — previous balls shift the arm positions, changing routes for future balls. Center of the board. Printed in gradient purple-to-teal
- **THE VOLCANO (×1):** A tall cone at the top-center. Balls spiral up the outside on a helical track, drop into the caldera, and erupt from one of 3 random exit chutes at the base. Printed in red-to-orange gradient. The "how did it end up THERE?!" moment
- **THE ARCHIMEDES CRANK (×2, at launcher stations):** The player-operated ball lift. A hand crank drives a helical screw inside a tube. Turning the crank lifts balls from the collection trough to the launch point. Speed of cranking = launch cadence. These are BIG prints — 18" tall, assembled from 4 interlocking sections

### Win Condition
- **60-second round.** Crank as fast as you can, launch as many balls as possible
- Ball speed through the contraption varies wildly — some balls take 3 seconds (straight drops), some take 15+ seconds (gear ratchets, octopus loops)
- Balls from BOTH players interact with the SAME mechanisms — your ball can catapult your opponent's ball into the jackpot (or the toilet)
- **Competitive:** Colored balls (red vs blue), highest score wins
- **Chaos mode:** All balls same color, combined score, beat the house target

## What Makes It Special

1. **THE PRINTERS ARE THE GAME.** Every mechanism is a conversation piece. Parents lean in. "Wait, you *printed* that?" Kids can see gears meshing, pendulums swinging, screws turning. It's a working museum of mechanical engineering disguised as a carnival game
2. **Every run is different.** Stateful mechanisms mean the board literally changes between balls. Pendulums alternate. See-saws load and fire. Gear wheels advance. Two identical launches produce completely different results because of what happened 3 balls ago
3. **Cross-player interaction.** Both players' balls share the same mechanisms. Your ball loads a see-saw, your opponent's ball triggers it. The octopus arms shift after every ball. It's cooperative chaos even in competitive mode
4. **Modular & immortal.** Standard bolt pattern means mechanisms swap in 30 seconds. Break the octopus arm? Print a new one overnight. Want to add a new mechanism next year? Design it, print it, bolt it on. The game EVOLVES
5. **The crank.** There's something deeply satisfying about a hand crank. It's tactile, it's physical, it's steampunk. Kids feel like they're operating a MACHINE, not pressing a button. The Archimedes screw visibly carrying balls upward is mesmerizing on its own
6. **Visual density.** 25+ unique mechanisms in neon colors against a matte black backboard under clear acrylic. Every square inch has something happening. It's a kinetic sculpture that you play. Spectators can watch for 10 minutes and still notice new interactions
7. **The print farm story.** Display the two Bambu printers at the booth, still churning out spare parts. A sign: "Every piece on this board was printed right here." Kids realize they're looking at the future and lose their minds

## Vibe
Think: a Rube Goldberg machine meets a pinball machine meets a Willy Wonka factory. The sheer density of *things happening* — gears turning, pendulums swinging, balls spiraling through helixes — creates a sense of wonder that plain pegs can never match. This isn't just a game. It's a spectacle. It's a statement. It's two dads and two printers saying "watch what we built" and every kid in line thinking "I want to build stuff like that."

## Print Schedule (2 Bambu Labs printers, 8 weeks)

### Weeks 1-2: Passive Mechanisms
- 4 spinning wheels (2 hrs each = 8 hrs)
- 3 funnel spirals (3 hrs each = 9 hrs)
- 6 bumper mushrooms (1.5 hrs each = 9 hrs)
- 2 helix drops (3 hrs each = 6 hrs)
- **Total: ~32 hrs across 2 printers = ~2 days of printing**

### Weeks 3-4: Stateful Machines
- 3 pendulum gates (6 hrs each = 18 hrs)
- 2 see-saw catapults (8 hrs each = 16 hrs)
- 2 gear ratchets (8 hrs each = 16 hrs)
- 3 trap doors (5 hrs each = 15 hrs)
- **Total: ~65 hrs = ~4 days of printing**

### Weeks 5-6: Showstoppers + Scoring
- 1 octopus (20 hrs, multi-part)
- 1 volcano (15 hrs, multi-part)
- 2 Archimedes cranks (12 hrs each = 24 hrs, 4 sections each)
- 11 scoring buckets (2 hrs each = 22 hrs)
- **Total: ~81 hrs = ~5 days of printing**

### Weeks 7-8: Spares, Refinements, Test Prints
- Reprint any mechanisms that need design tweaks after playtesting
- Print spare parts for high-wear mechanisms (pendulums, trap door springs)
- Print decorative elements (sign letters, border trim, emoji faces for scoring buckets)
- **Buffer for iteration — this is where the magic happens**

**Total estimated print time: ~200-250 hrs. With 2 printers, that's ~125 hrs each — roughly 5 days of continuous printing per printer spread across 8 weeks. PLENTY of margin for iteration and reprints.**
