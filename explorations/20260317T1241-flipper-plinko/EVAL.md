# Flipper Plinko — Carnival Game Evaluation

## Scoring Rubric
Each heuristic scored 1–10 (10 = best possible).

---

### Overall Entertainment: 8/10
The flipper mechanic transforms passive plinko ("drop and watch") into active pinball ("drop, watch, SAVE"). Every ball that reaches the bottom becomes a moment of tension — will you time the squeeze right? The satisfying *thwack* of a rubber-band-sprung flipper launching a ball back into the neon peg forest is addictive. It's not as physically explosive as stomping or cannon-blasting, but the skill loop (launch → cascade → flip → cascade again) creates a dopamine rhythm that keeps kids locked in. The combo scoring system ("keep it alive for 3 passes and it doubles") rewards persistence and creates hero moments.

### Memorability: 7/10
"Remember that plinko game where you could hit the balls back up?" — solid, but less instantly iconic than "tornado cannons" or "stomping on the ground." The flipper mechanic is familiar from pinball, which helps onboarding but reduces novelty. What IS memorable: the neon peg forest on black pegboard looks incredible, and the *thwack-cascade-thwack* rhythm creates a distinctive audio signature. The portability story ("two guys carried the whole game in from a Honda Civic") becomes its own legend among the parent volunteers.

### Ease of Construction: 9/10
This is where Flipper Plinko destroys the competition. No plywood. No acrylic cutting. No lumber. No heavy assembly.
- **Pegboard:** $12, pre-drilled, cuts with a jigsaw
- **Frame:** PVC pipes press into 3D-printed connectors
- **Pegs:** Batch-printed overnight, push into holes
- **Buckets:** Print and hook on
- **Flippers:** The only real build challenge — brake cable routing and pivot bolts

Two dads could build this across a couple of relaxed evenings, with the 3D printer doing most of the work while they sleep. Total cost ~$200 vs $350-400 for the OG. The real flex: assembly on carnival day takes 10 minutes with zero tools.

### Skill to Luck Spectrum: 8/10 (Excellent balance)
**Skill factors:** Flipper timing is genuine skill — too early or late and the ball drains. Launch strength matters (soft launch = lower pegs, hard launch = reaches the jackpot zone but more unpredictable). Choosing when to go for combos vs. safe single-pass scores is strategic.
**Luck factors:** Once the ball enters the peg field, it's pure plinko chaos. No amount of skill controls which bucket it lands in. Ball-on-ball collisions when multiple are in play add randomness.
The beautiful thing: skill determines HOW MANY CHANCES each ball gets (via flippers), but luck determines WHAT IT SCORES each pass. Skilled players win more often, but any kid can get a lucky cascade into the 100-point jackpot on their first try.

### Chaos Level: 6/10
Honestly, the lowest chaos score of the three explorations. The peg cascade is visually exciting but contained — balls move in predictable downward paths with random lateral bounces. The flipper adds drama but not pandemonium. There's no air blasting balls sideways, no full-body stomping, no vortex cannons. It's controlled, rhythmic chaos rather than all-out bedlam. That said, when both players have 3 balls cascading simultaneously and are frantically squeezing flippers, the board becomes a neon pinball fever dream.

### Durability: 9/10
**Strong points:** Pegboard is cheap and disposable — if it gets damaged, $12 replacement. PVC frame is nearly indestructible. 3D-printed PETG pegs can take hundreds of ball impacts. Polycarbonate front is shatter-proof.
**Weak points:** Rubber bands on flippers will fatigue — keep a bag of spares ($3). Brake cables could stretch over time — easy to adjust or replace ($5). One 3D-printed peg might snap if a ball hits it at just the wrong angle — swap in a spare in 2 seconds.
**The killer advantage:** EVERYTHING is modular. Pull a broken peg, push in a new one. Swap a rubber band. Unhook a damaged bucket and hook on a new one. No screws to unscrew, no glue to undo. This game was designed to be repaired mid-carnival by a volunteer with zero tools.

### Photogenicity: 8/10
Neon pegs on matte black pegboard is visually striking — the board looks like a retro arcade screenshot. The clear polycarbonate front means balls are visible from any angle. Action shots of a kid squeezing the brake lever while a ball cascades through rainbow pegs will photograph well. The compact 4×5 ft size means the whole game fits in a single phone frame.
Deducting points because the flipper action happens at the bottom of the board (less visible to spectators standing behind the player) and the game lacks a single WOW visual element like a glowing neon peg forest (no LEDs in the base build — though adding a $15 LED strip behind the pegboard would bump this to 10/10).

### Onboarding Time: 9/10 (Instant — pinball is universal)
**15-second explanation:** "Pull the plunger to launch the ball. Watch it bounce through the pegs. If it drops to the bottom, squeeze this handle to flip it back up. Score as many points as you can. Go."
Every kid has seen pinball — the flipper concept is immediately understood. The pegboard plinko cascade is self-explanatory. The only learning curve is flipper timing, which takes exactly one ball to calibrate. Scoring is printed on the buckets. No ambiguity.

## Verdict
Flipper Plinko trades raw spectacle for practical excellence. It won't draw a crowd from across the field like Stomp Rockets (8.8) or Tornado Alley (8.1), but it will be the game that actually gets built, actually gets transported without a truck, actually gets set up in 10 minutes, and actually survives 5 years of carnivals without needing a rebuild.

The flipper mechanic is the right amount of innovation — familiar enough that every kid gets it instantly, novel enough in a plinko context that it feels fresh. The combo scoring system creates natural strategy and replay desire.

**The real score that matters:** Flipper Plinko is the only concept where two dads can build it on weeknights, carry it in one trip, set it up before the other booths finish unpacking, and fix any broken part in under 60 seconds. At a school carnival, logistics IS the game.

**Biggest risk:** The flipper pneumatics — er, mechanics. The brake-lever-to-cable-to-flipper chain has multiple failure points. Prototype one flipper assembly first and abuse-test it with 500 squeezes before committing to the full build. If the cable routing through PVC pipes creates too much friction, switch to external cable routing with 3D-printed guides.

**Biggest upgrade opportunity:** Swap the standard pegboard for ¼" white pegboard and add RGB LED strips behind it. The pegs cast shadows, the board glows, and Flipper Plinko becomes a neon beacon at evening carnivals. $15 in LEDs turns an 8/10 photogenicity into an 11/10.
