# BLOWER DUEL PLINKO — Carnival Game Evaluation

## Scoring (1–10 scale)

### Overall Entertainment: 9.5/10
The core loop — drop, decide, blast — has three distinct strategic choices every single drop. The face blast mechanic alone would carry a lesser game. Combined with actual ball-steering skill and the sabotage option, this is a game that stays interesting for dozens of plays. The simultaneous play means there's never a dull moment waiting for your turn. Both players are always engaged, always making decisions, always reacting.

### Memorability: 10/10
"Remember when Tyler blasted me in the face and my ball went in the jackpot anyway?" This game generates stories. The face blast is inherently hilarious and visually dramatic — hair flying, eyes squinting, everyone laughing. Every game produces at least one moment the crowd reacts to. The social dynamics (choosing to help yourself vs. attack your friend) create narratives that kids retell. Mom caught it on video. Dad is still laughing about it at dinner.

### Ease of Construction: 7.5/10
Simpler than the original Air Plinko in some ways (no acrylic panel, no bouncy house blower, no hose routing). The hardware cloth mesh is actually easier to work with than acrylic — no drilling precise holes through brittle material. The frame is straightforward 2×4 construction. The main complexity is ensuring the mesh is taut and the pegs span both layers properly. Two weekends is realistic. Estimated cost ~$290 (excluding M18 tools already owned) is very reasonable. The biggest challenge: getting the air port funnels dialed so the blast spreads nicely across the field.

### Skill to Luck Spectrum: 7/10 Skill, 3/10 Luck
Heavily skill-weighted, which is unusual (and good) for a carnival game. Players control:
- Which drop slot to use
- When to blast and which port to use
- Whether to steer, sabotage, or face blast
- Timing (blast when the ball is in the right zone)

The luck comes from peg bounces — classic plinko randomness. But skilled players will consistently outscore unskilled ones because air placement matters enormously. The meta-game (reading your opponent, faking a face blast then steering your ball) adds a poker-like psychological dimension.

### Chaos Level: 9/10
Simultaneous play + crosswinds from opposing blasts = absolute bedlam. When both players blast at the same time, balls go in directions neither player intended. The face blast adds physical comedy chaos — a player flinching mid-aim sends their own air stream wild. Multiple balls in the field at once (since play is simultaneous) means the board is alive with movement. The sound of two M18 blowers firing at once is viscerally chaotic.

### Durability: 8.5/10
Hardware cloth is nearly indestructible — far more durable than acrylic which can crack. The 2×4 frame is tank-like. Whiffle balls are cheap and replaceable. The M18 blowers are professional tools designed for jobsite abuse. Weakest points: the batting cage netting (may need replacement after a few events) and the rubber gaskets on air ports (cheap and swappable). The trap door ball release mechanism has moving parts that could jam — keep it simple (gravity + a pull rod). Overall, this is a game built from construction materials designed to take abuse.

### Photogenicity: 9.5/10
The money shot: two kids facing each other through a mesh of pegs and flying balls, one blasting the other in the face, hair horizontal, both laughing. This photo takes itself. The red Milwaukee tools against the dark frame pop on camera. The mesh creates an interesting visual texture — you can see through it to the opponent, giving photos depth. The M18 tower light on top acts as a visual landmark. The scoring zones in bright colors photograph well. Night photos with the tower light glowing would be exceptional.

### Onboarding Time: 8/10 (Fast)
**Explanation needed:** "Drop a ball. Use the blower to steer it. Or blast your opponent. Highest score wins."

That's it. 10 seconds. A kid can understand the controls (point blower, pull trigger) instantly because they're intuitive. The three-way decision (steer/sabotage/face blast) reveals itself naturally through play — first-time players default to steering, then discover face blasting, then develop sabotage strategies. No rules explanation needed for the face blast — the first time someone does it, the other player immediately understands the game has a new dimension.

**Compared to cornhole:** Slightly more complex (blower adds a decision layer) but equally intuitive physically.
**Compared to D&D:** Light-years simpler. Zero rules to memorize.

## What Pushes This Over the Top

The face blast mechanic transforms this from "good carnival game" to "the thing every kid at school talks about Monday morning." It's the social decision that matters: you're not just playing a game, you're choosing whether to be a hero or a villain, and your opponent is standing right there watching you decide. That psychological layer — visible through the mesh, 3 feet away, both holding power tools — is what makes this unforgettable.

The fact that face blasting is a *suboptimal strategy* (your ball falls unguided while you're attacking) but an *irresistible one* is the tension that makes it sing. The best players learn to steer. But the most memorable moments come from the chaos.
