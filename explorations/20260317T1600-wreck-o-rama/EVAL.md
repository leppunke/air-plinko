# WRECK-O-RAMA — Carnival Game Evaluation

## Overall Entertainment: 9/10
Pure destruction is one of the most universally satisfying human experiences. Every kid (and every adult) wants to swing a wrecking ball at something. The rotating turntable adds just enough unpredictability to keep it interesting, and the block-scoring system gives it structure without killing the chaos. The sound of 40+ wooden blocks cascading is a dopamine hit you can hear from three booths away. Docked one point because it's a single-action game (one swing) — there's no extended gameplay loop within a single turn.

## Memorability: 10/10
"Remember that game where you swung a wrecking ball and smashed a tower?" Yes. Yes they do. This is the game kids describe to their friends on Monday morning. The visual spectacle of blocks flying, the satisfying crash, the competitive scoring, the skull blocks that cost you points — every element creates a story. The crown block mechanic (500 pts only if it lands in the crown zone) creates those one-in-a-hundred "NO WAY" moments that become legends.

## Ease of Construction: 8/10
This is straightforward carpentry:
- **Gantry:** Three 4×4 posts bolted together in an inverted U. Diagonal braces for stability. Any dad with a drill can build this in a morning.
- **Turntable:** Two plywood circles with a lazy susan bearing. Dead simple.
- **Blocks:** Rip 1×4s to width, crosscut, sand, paint. Repetitive but easy. Great "beer and bandsaw" afternoon project.
- **Wrecking ball:** Foam ball in a bag on a rope. Ten minutes.
- **No electronics.** No plumbing. No air systems. No moving parts except the bearing and the rope.
- Docked two points because the gantry is 8 ft tall and requires a friend to assemble safely.

## Skill-to-Luck Spectrum: 6/10 (Balanced, leaning skill)
- **Skill elements:** Swing timing (turntable rotation), swing angle (hitting the weak side vs strong side), swing force (controlled vs wild), reading the tower structure (where are the gold blocks buried?)
- **Luck elements:** How the tower crumbles is inherently chaotic and unpredictable. Where the crown block lands is largely physics/luck. Block placement varies between turns.
- A skilled player can consistently outscore a random swinger, but the chaos of the collapse means upsets happen regularly. Good balance.

## Chaos Level: 9/10
Forty wooden blocks exploding off a spinning turntable. Blocks flying into collection walls. The CRASH echoing across the carnival. Flying block debris. The turntable still spinning with a few stubborn blocks standing. A skull block landing face-up right next to you. The crown tumbling through the air and JUST missing the crown zone. This is controlled demolition and it's beautiful.

## Durability: 9/10
- **Gantry:** 4×4 lumber. Built to last decades. Wing nut joints mean easy disassembly/reassembly year after year.
- **Blocks:** Pine blocks will get scuffed and chipped over time, but they're trivially cheap to replace ($4 for a new 1×4 yields 15 blocks). Paint touch-ups are 10 minutes.
- **Turntable:** Lazy susan bearing is a wear item but they're $15 and swappable in 5 minutes.
- **Wrecking ball:** Foam ball in a canvas bag. Will last multiple events. Rope might need replacing annually.
- **Collection walls:** Cheapest component, easiest to replace.
- No fragile components. No electronics to fry. No acrylic to crack. No motors to burn out.

## Photogenicity: 10/10
This is made for cameras:
- **Action shot:** Ball mid-swing, blocks mid-explosion. Slow-motion phone video of the tower crumbling. Every parent will film this.
- **Setup shot:** The tower standing tall on the spinning turntable with the wrecking ball hanging dramatically. Looks impressive from 50 feet away.
- **Aftermath shot:** The carnage. Blocks scattered everywhere. One gold block landed in the crown zone.
- **The game draws a crowd.** It's loud, visual, dramatic. People see it happening and walk over. Natural crowd-builder.
- The gantry frame creates a natural "stage" framing the action.

## Onboarding Time: 10/10 (Instant)
"Grab the ball. Swing it at the tower. Blocks off the table count. Gold is good. Skulls are bad. GO."

Five seconds. Maybe three. There is zero rule ambiguity. A 4-year-old understands this game. A 40-year-old understands this game. The blocks have colors. You smash them. Done.

No practice swings needed. No "wait, how does this work?" No instruction card. No operator explanation beyond "SWING!"

## Why This Breaks the Mold

Every other exploration in this project is a plinko variant — balls falling through pegs into scoring zones. WRECK-O-RAMA has **zero plinko DNA**:

- No pegs. No grid. No falling-through-obstacles mechanic.
- Instead of gravity pulling balls DOWN through chaos, a wrecking ball moves HORIZONTALLY through a structure.
- Instead of watching passively after launch, the player IS the launch mechanism — full-body, one decisive moment.
- Instead of balls settling into scoring buckets, blocks EXPLODE outward and scoring is about what leaves the platform.
- The game is about **destruction**, not **navigation**.
- Reset is human-powered (stacking blocks) not gravity-fed.
- The rotating turntable adds a timing element that no plinko game has.

This is a carnival game in the truest tradition — a test of skill wrapped in spectacle, designed to separate kids from their tickets and leave them grinning.
