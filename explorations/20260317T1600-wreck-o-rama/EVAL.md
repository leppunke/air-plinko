# WRECK-O-RAMA — Carnival Game Evaluation

## Overall Entertainment: 6/10
Swinging a wrecking ball at a tower of blocks is satisfying. The rotating turntable adds a timing element that elevates it above a static knock-down game. But the fundamental problem is severe: this is a single-action game. The kid swings once, watches blocks fall for two seconds, and their turn is over. Total active play time is roughly three seconds. Then someone has to restack 40+ blocks before the next kid can play — a process that takes 60+ seconds even if you're fast. At a 15-kid line, the ratio of waiting to playing is brutal. The destruction moment is great; the other 95% of the experience (waiting, watching someone stack blocks) is not.

## Memorability: 7/10
Tower knock-down games exist at every county fair and carnival in America. Stacked-can smash, ball-toss pyramids, and block-topple games are among the most common carnival formats. Wreck-o-Rama is a well-executed version of this established format — the turntable and themed blocks add polish — but it is not a novel invention. A parent volunteer who has attended three school carnivals has seen a knock-down game before. The crown block mechanic adds a nice scoring wrinkle, but it doesn't make this a fundamentally new experience.

## Ease of Construction: 8/10
This is straightforward carpentry:
- **Gantry:** Three 4x4 posts bolted together in an inverted U. Diagonal braces for stability.
- **Turntable:** Two plywood circles with a lazy susan bearing.
- **Blocks:** Rip 1x4s to width, crosscut, sand, paint. Repetitive but easy.
- **Wrecking ball:** Foam ball in a bag on a rope. Ten minutes.
- **No electronics.** No plumbing. No air systems. No moving parts except the bearing and the rope.
- The gantry is 8 ft tall and requires a second person to assemble safely.

## Skill-to-Luck Spectrum: 6/10 (Balanced, leaning skill)
- **Skill elements:** Swing timing (turntable rotation), swing angle, swing force, reading the tower structure.
- **Luck elements:** How the tower crumbles is chaotic. Where the crown block lands is mostly luck. Block placement varies between turns.
- A skilled player can consistently outscore a random swinger, but the chaos of the collapse means upsets happen regularly.

## Chaos Level: 9/10
Forty wooden blocks exploding off a spinning turntable is genuinely loud and dramatic. Blocks scatter, the turntable keeps spinning with a few stubborn survivors, and the crash is audible from several booths away. The destruction spectacle is the concept's strongest asset.

## Durability: 9/10
- **Gantry:** 4x4 lumber with wing nut joints for easy disassembly.
- **Blocks:** Pine blocks will get scuffed but are trivially cheap to replace.
- **Turntable:** Lazy susan bearing is a wear item but cheap and swappable.
- **Wrecking ball:** Foam ball in a canvas bag. Rope may need annual replacement.
- No fragile components. No electronics. No motors.

## Photogenicity: 7/10
The destruction moment — ball mid-swing, blocks mid-explosion — is genuinely camera-worthy and every parent will want to film it. However, that moment lasts about two seconds per player. The other 95% of the game's visual state is someone crouching over the turntable restacking 40+ blocks. A game that looks like a cleanup operation most of the time is not a 10 on photogenicity.

## Onboarding Time: 8/10
The basic action — swing ball at tower — is instantly understood. But the scoring system adds friction: gold blocks are good, skull blocks subtract points, the crown block scores 500 only if it lands in the crown zone. "Blocks off the table count. Gold is good. Skulls are bad. Crown in the zone is a bonus." That is four rules, not zero. Still fast by carnival standards, but not "instant" when compared to games like "throw the bean bag at the hole."

---

## Throughput Problem (Critical Weakness)

This is the dealbreaker that the original eval glossed over. Every player turn requires a full tower reset: 40+ blocks, carefully stacked in a specific configuration (gold blocks hidden, skulls placed, crown on top). At a 15-kid line, that is 600+ individual block placements over the course of the line. Who does this?

- A parent volunteer restacking blocks all day will burn out by lunch.
- If kids "help" restack, the tower quality degrades and setup takes even longer.
- A sloppy tower falls over before the player swings, which is embarrassing and wastes more time.
- Estimated throughput: one player every 90 seconds (3s swing + 60-90s reset). That is 40 players per hour. For a busy carnival booth, this is poor.

**Mitigation options exist** (pre-stacked trays, multiple turntables on rotation, simplified tower designs with fewer blocks), but they all add construction complexity and cost. The base concept as described has a serious operational problem.

## Verdict

Wreck-o-Rama is a solid knock-down game. The turntable rotation is the one genuinely novel element, adding a timing skill layer that static knock-down games lack. The destruction spectacle is real and the construction is approachable.

But it is a knock-down game — a format that already exists at most carnivals. And it has a throughput problem that no amount of enthusiasm can wave away. One swing, then a 60-90 second reset, repeated hundreds of times across a carnival day. The person restacking blocks has the worst volunteer job at the event.

**Build it if:** you have a dedicated volunteer who genuinely wants to stack blocks for four hours, OR you invest in the pre-stacked tray system (which adds meaningful build complexity). The turntable makes it the best version of this game format.

**Don't build it if:** you're short on volunteers, expect high throughput, or have other concepts that offer longer active play time per player. A game where the kid plays for three seconds and a volunteer works for sixty is an unfavorable ratio.
