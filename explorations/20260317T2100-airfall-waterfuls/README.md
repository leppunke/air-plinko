# AIRFALL

## The Pitch

Remember Waterfuls? That handheld water game where you pumped buttons to jet rings onto little pegs? Now picture it eight feet tall, filled with ping pong balls instead of water, and powered by two M18 Milwaukee leaf blowers screaming at full throttle.

**AIRFALL** is a two-player carnival game where each player aims a real cordless leaf blower through a port at the bottom of a tall acrylic chamber and tries to blast ping pong balls upward onto scoring shelves. The balls are so light they hover, swirl, and dance in the air streams — drifting up like bubbles in water, colliding mid-air, piling onto ledges, cascading back down in waterfalls of white and color when a shelf overflows. It's the Waterfuls toy scaled to 10x, the water replaced with hurricane-force wind, and the gentle button pump replaced with a tool that could strip paint off a deck.

## How It Plays

### Setup
Forty ping pong balls sit in a trough at the bottom of a tall enclosed chamber. Two blower nozzle ports — one on each side — poke through the bottom edge of the cabinet at waist height. Two M18 Milwaukee blowers hang from the frame on bungee tethers. A volunteer hands each player a blower.

### Gameplay (60 seconds)

A volunteer starts a countdown timer. Players squeeze the triggers.

The blowers roar to life. Air screams through the nozzle ports into the chamber. Ping pong balls launch off the bottom trough and rocket upward. Players sweep their nozzle aim left and right, up and down, trying to push balls onto the scoring shelves mounted at different heights inside the chamber.

The balls don't just fly — they *float*. A ping pong ball weighs 2.7 grams. An M18 blower pushes 450 CFM. The balls hover in the air column, drift sideways, collide with each other, stack on ledges, and get knocked off by other balls being pushed up from below. A shelf fills up with 6 balls, a 7th one hits, and 4 cascade off in a mini waterfall — exactly like the Waterfuls toy when you pushed too hard.

The two air streams interact. Players' jets collide in the center, creating turbulence zones where balls spin in lazy circles. A skilled player learns to pulse the trigger — short bursts for precision placement, sustained blast to push balls to the highest shelves.

The round ends. Balls settle. A volunteer counts what's on each shelf.

### Scoring

Shelves are mounted at five heights, alternating sides:

```
         [100] ← Top center, tiny shelf, brutal to reach
        /     \
    [50]       [50] ← Upper shelves, medium
      |         |
    [25]       [25] ← Mid shelves, wider
      |         |
    [10]       [10] ← Low shelves, easiest, widest
```

**Player A** scores for balls resting on left-side shelves. **Player B** scores right-side shelves. The top-center 100-point shelf is contested — both players can score it, but both players' air streams destabilize it.

Colored balls add spice: 30 white balls (1x), 6 red balls (2x), 4 gold balls (3x). Multipliers apply to whatever shelf they land on.

### Reset (~15 seconds)

A volunteer opens a small trapdoor on each shelf (pull-tabs on the back). All balls cascade down to the bottom trough. Trapdoors close. Next two players step up. Blowers are tethered to the frame — no hand-off logistics.

**Cycle time: ~90 seconds per pair.**

## What Makes This Different

1. **The Waterfuls effect at carnival scale.** Ping pong balls floating in air streams look like rings drifting in water. It's hypnotic. No other carnival game creates this visual — balls hovering, drifting sideways, gently settling onto shelves, then cascading off in waterfalls when disturbed. It triggers the exact same satisfaction as the handheld toy but at a scale that draws a crowd.

2. **Real tools, real power.** Kids get to use actual Milwaukee M18 blowers. The weight, the trigger, the ROAR. This isn't a button or a lever — it's a power tool. For a 7-year-old, holding a leaf blower and making ping pong balls fly is a core memory. For the dads in the crowd, watching their M18s finally do something fun is a different kind of core memory.

3. **Continuous control, not launch-and-watch.** Players are actively steering air for the full 60 seconds. Every micro-adjustment matters. There's no passive phase. Your hands are on the controls from start to finish, and the feedback is instant — you see the balls respond to your aim in real time.

4. **Physics create natural difficulty scaling.** Low shelves are easy to hit with a steady blast. Mid shelves require sweeping aim. The top shelf requires sustained upward thrust that destabilizes everything below it. Going for 100 points means risking the 25s and 50s you already banked. Kids discover this trade-off naturally.

5. **The cascade moment.** When a shelf gets bumped and 6 balls waterfall down through the field, disrupting everything below them, the whole crowd reacts. It's the Waterfuls "ohhh no" moment writ large. These cascades are the game's emotional punctuation — they happen organically, unpredictably, and everyone watching feels them.

## The Vibe

Two kids stand at the base of a towering clear cabinet, each gripping a yellow-and-black Milwaukee blower like they're about to clear a yard of leaves. A timer beeps. They squeeze the triggers. Twin hurricanes blast into the chamber and forty ping pong balls EXPLODE off the bottom and swirl upward in a chaotic cloud. Balls hover, drift, collide, settle on shelves, get knocked off by other balls, cascade down in mini waterfalls, get caught by a player's air stream and pushed back up. The noise is glorious — blower motors screaming, balls clattering against acrylic, crowd screaming louder. One kid figures out trigger pulsing and starts stacking balls on the 50-point shelf like a surgeon. The other kid goes full throttle at the 100-point shelf and accidentally creates a vortex that clears half the board. Gold balls are spinning in circles between the two air streams. The timer hits zero, balls rain down and settle, and both kids are already asking to go again.

That's AIRFALL.
