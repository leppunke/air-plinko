# BUZZ BALL — Carnival Game Evaluation

## Scoring (1–10 scale)

### Overall Entertainment: 7/10
The spring plunger gives satisfying tactile feedback, and balls cascading through hex pegs look good. The bee bumpers add genuine surprise when they fire. But the player's actual experience is thin: pull a plunger, then watch. The interesting agency belongs to the volunteer, not the player. Alternating turns means each kid is idle roughly half the play time — standing there watching the other player's ball bounce. For a 2-player game, that's a structural problem, not a minor dock. The brass bell is a nice moment, but one bell ring per game (if it happens at all) doesn't sustain entertainment across five balls of pull-and-watch.

### Memorability: 7/10
The hex-peg layout and honeycomb aesthetic are visually distinct — a parent or kid would recognize this isn't a standard plinko board. The volunteer-operated bumpers add a "something weird happened" moment. But from the player's perspective, the memory is: "I pulled a plunger and watched a ball bounce, and sometimes a bumper moved it." That's a twist on plinko, not a fundamentally new experience. The volunteer doing the cranking has the memorable role — the player is mostly a spectator of their own game. The bee theming is nice set dressing, but theming alone doesn't make a game stick in memory for years. Compared to a game where the kid is physically doing something novel the whole time, this is a tier below.

### Ease of Construction: 5/10
The base plinko board is straightforward lumber and plywood. The hex peg caps require a 3D printer (90 caps is a big batch but simple geometry). The scoring buckets are standard woodworking. **The bee bumper mechanism is the real problem.** Cam axles with 120-degree offsets, compression springs, pushrod alignment, and bushing installation — this is precision machining, not "moderate mechanical skill." If the cam timing is off, the bumpers either jam or fire weakly, and diagnosing that on carnival morning with 15 kids in line is a nightmare. "Two handy dads" understates what's needed; this wants someone comfortable with lathe work or very precise 3D printing with tight tolerances. Budget a full weekend just for bumper assembly and tuning, and expect at least one redesign cycle when the first attempt binds. The mechanism is the concept's biggest build risk.

### Appeal by Elementary School Grade: 8/10
| Grade | Appeal | Notes |
|-------|--------|-------|
| K–1 | 7/10 | Love watching the chaos. Plunger pull might be hard for smallest kids — may need a "helper pull" from parent. Bumper pops are exciting but can startle |
| 2–3 | 9/10 | Sweet spot. Strong enough to work the plunger, old enough to adjust pull strength, surprised by bumper pops |
| 4–5 | 8/10 | Competitive enough to care about score. Will experiment with pull strength. But the limited player agency (pull and watch) may feel passive to older kids who want to DO more |
| Adults | 7/10 | Pleasant to play once. The bumper randomness prevents "solving" it. But adults will quickly notice they're mostly watching, not playing |

### Skill to Luck Spectrum: 8/10 (good balance)
- **Skill component:** Pull strength on the plunger controls launch height. Gentle pull = safer low-zone pegs, easy 50/100 pts. Full pull = high-zone with more chaos, access to 200/500 but also more bumper exposure. Learning curve is fast and rewarding
- **Luck component:** Hex peg bounces are inherently chaotic (6-directional vs. 2-directional on square grids). Bee bumpers add massive randomness — you can't predict when the volunteer cranks. Ball paths through the field are genuinely unpredictable
- **The balance:** A skilled player scores higher by choosing smart pull strengths, but anyone can get lucky with a bumper-assisted Queen's Cell hit. A 2nd grader can beat a 5th grader, which is the right dynamic for a carnival game

### Chaos Level: 6/10
Hex geometry does create more bounce angles than a square grid — that's real. But at any given moment, there is one ball on the board, falling through pegs with an occasional bumper pop. That's not chaos — it's a single ball taking a mildly unpredictable path. "Three bee bumpers popping in sequence can send multiple balls flying simultaneously" contradicts the alternating-turns format acknowledged elsewhere: with one ball at a time, you get one ball bouncing off one bumper. The volunteer adds some surprise, but the chaos ceiling is limited by the one-ball-at-a-time constraint. Compare to a game where multiple things are happening simultaneously and the player is actively contributing to the disorder — this is calmer than it wants to be.

### Durability: 7/10
- **Board & frame:** Plywood + lumber + carriage bolts. Solid — no concerns here
- **Hex peg caps:** 3D printed PLA. Will crack over time from repeated ball impacts. Replaceable, but 90 caps means you need to keep a stock of spares and check regularly
- **Acrylic front:** Shatter-resistant 1/4". Fine unless someone drops the board during transport
- **Bee bumpers:** The real durability concern. Springs fatigue after heavy use. Cam surfaces wear against pushrods, introducing slop that changes the bumper timing. This is a moving-parts mechanism being operated continuously by a volunteer who may crank too hard — wear is not theoretical, it's guaranteed. Pre-carnival maintenance is not 30 minutes; it's disassembly, inspection, and recalibration
- **Plunger assemblies:** Standard pinball parts, should hold up well
- **Overall:** The static parts of this game are durable. The cam mechanism — the feature that makes it interesting — is the part most likely to degrade or fail

### Photogenicity: 8/10
Gold-and-black honeycomb on a 6-foot board is genuinely high-contrast and reads as intentional from a distance. Clear acrylic front allows photos of balls mid-fall. Yellow balls against dark honeycomb will photograph well. The hex geometry looks designed rather than DIY. The volunteer in a beekeeper hat adds a visual hook. Docked because: the board's back side (where the cam mechanism lives) is an exposed mess of hardware, and the player's action — pulling a plunger — is not particularly photogenic. The interesting visual moment is the ball path, which is fast and hard to capture on a phone camera.

### Onboarding Time: 8/10 (fast)
- **Explain in one sentence:** "Pull the plunger, launch the ball, highest score wins"
- **Rules kids need to know:** Pull harder = ball goes higher = more risk and reward. 5 balls each, take turns
- **What they discover on their own:** The bumpers fire unpredictably. This is genuinely self-evident once it happens
- **Zero learning curve on the plunger** — pull and release is intuitive
- **Signage:** Point values are on the buckets
- Docked because: the alternating-turn structure requires explaining "wait for your turn" to excited kids, and the volunteer's role may need a brief explanation to the next parent taking over the booth. Not complicated, but not quite cornhole-level simplicity

## Verdict
Buzz Ball's genuine strength is the volunteer interaction mechanic and the hex-peg geometry — those are real differentiators from a standard plinko board. The honeycomb aesthetic looks good and will stand out visually at the carnival.

The honest weakness is player agency. The player pulls a plunger and watches. The volunteer behind the board is the one doing the interesting thing. A kid describing this game says "I pulled a thing and a ball bounced around and sometimes a bumper moved it." That's a fun plinko variant, not a generational carnival memory.

**Build it if:** you have a builder comfortable with precision mechanical work (cam axles, spring calibration) and a reliable, energetic volunteer committed to working the bumpers all day. The volunteer makes or breaks this game — a bored parent cranking half-heartedly turns this into a standard plinko board with set dressing.

**Don't build it if:** your build team tops out at basic woodworking, or you can't guarantee a dedicated bumper operator. Without an engaged volunteer, the core differentiator disappears and you're left with a harder-to-build plinko board that scores about the same as an easier one.
