# WHACK ATTACK

## Concept
Manual two-player whack-a-mole. A 4ft × 3ft board stands vertically with 12 holes cut in it. Player One hides behind the board and shoves foam-topped plungers through the holes — pop! pop! pop! — in random patterns. Player Two stands in front with a foam mallet and WHACKS the targets back through before they disappear. Roles swap after each round. Most whacks wins.

No electronics. No motors. No programming. Just one kid popping targets and another kid smashing them. The "AI" is your friend. The chaos is real. The laughter is nuclear.

## The Pitch
*"WHACK THE MOLES! Your partner pops 'em up — you smack 'em down! Then you switch! Most whacks wins! Ready? WHACK ATTACK!"*

## Gameplay

### Setup
- Whack board: 48"W × 36"H ¾" plywood, standing vertical on a sturdy A-frame base
- 12 holes: 4" diameter, arranged in a 4×3 grid with 10" spacing
- 12 foam plungers: Dowel sticks (18" long) with foam ball heads (3.5" diameter, fits through 4" holes)
- 1 foam mallet: Pool noodle on a dowel handle, or foam-padded bat
- Scoring: Each successful whack (target pushed back through before the "mole operator" withdraws it) = 1 point
- Round timer: 30 seconds per round
- The board is painted with mole holes, grass, dirt — classic whack-a-mole theming

### The Plungers (Moles)
- 18" wooden dowels (½" diameter) with a foam sphere glued on the end
- The foam head is 3.5" diameter — just smaller than the 4" hole, so it pops through cleanly
- The "mole operator" (Player behind the board) holds a plunger in each hand
- They shove a plunger through a hole, hold it for 1–2 seconds, then pull it back
- The operator moves around behind the board, choosing holes randomly
- The foam heads are painted as mole faces (googly eyes optional, highly recommended)
- When whacked, the plunger is pushed back through the hole — the operator feels the impact and knows it was a hit
- The mole operator keeps their own count of successful whacks (honor system or a parent/volunteer keeps score)

### Rounds
1. **Round 1 (30 sec):** Player A operates moles, Player B whacks
2. **Round 2 (30 sec):** Swap — Player B operates, Player A whacks
3. **Scoring:** Each player's whack count from their whacking round is their score. Highest total wins
4. **Tiebreaker:** 15-second lightning round — both players know the other's score, the pressure is ON

### The Meta-Game
Here's where it gets DEVIOUS. The mole operator controls the difficulty:
- **Easy mode:** Pop one mole at a time, hold it for 2 seconds, use the same few holes
- **Hard mode:** Pop two moles simultaneously (one in each hand), hold for only 1 second, use all 12 holes, move fast
- **Evil mode:** Fake-outs — start pushing a plunger through, pull it back before it's fully out. Feints. Double-pumps. The operator becomes an adversary

The operator WANTS the whacker to fail (because they're competing). So operators naturally escalate difficulty. This creates an arms race: the better the whacker, the trickier the operator gets. The game self-balances through human competitive instinct.

### Skill Progression
- **Beginner whackers:** Swing wildly, miss half the time, laugh hysterically
- **Intermediate:** Track patterns, anticipate which holes the operator favors, pre-position the mallet
- **Expert:** Read the operator's body language (feet visible under the board), predict the next hole, react in under half a second
- **Beginner operators:** Pop one mole, wait, pop another. Predictable
- **Expert operators:** Rapid-fire two at once, feints, never use the same hole twice in a row, vary timing to break the whacker's rhythm

## What Makes It Special

1. **TWO GAMES IN ONE** — You play BOTH roles. Whacking is fast-twitch reaction. Operating is strategy and deception. Most carnival games have one mechanic. This has two completely different skill sets and every player does both. The kid who's a great whacker might be a terrible operator. The quiet strategic kid might be the best mole operator anyone's ever seen.

2. **HUMAN-POWERED DIFFICULTY** — No difficulty dial. No settings. The other player IS the difficulty setting. Against a little kid? The moles are slow and predictable. Against the 5th-grade champion? The moles are lightning-fast feinting nightmares. The game automatically matches difficulty to the players because the OPPONENT is the difficulty engine. This is genius game design — no electronics could match the adaptiveness of a motivated 9-year-old.

3. **THE LAUGHTER FACTOR** — This game generates MORE laughter per minute than anything in the lineup. The frantic whacking, the misses, the fake-outs, the foam-on-foam BONK sound, the operator cackling behind the board, the whacker's frustrated flailing — it's physical comedy on par with a cartoon. Parents will be doubled over. Other kids in line will be laughing before it's even their turn.

4. **THE CHEAPEST BUILD** — Plywood, dowels, foam balls, a pool noodle. That's it. No PVC, no acrylic, no springs, no 3D printing, no paint beyond cosmetic decoration. Estimated total: **$40–50.** A single dad could build this in 3 hours on a Saturday morning. This is the highest fun-to-cost ratio in the lineup by a mile.

5. **ZERO ONBOARDING** — "Hit the things that pop out." Done. A 4-year-old understands. A 40-year-old understands. Nobody has ever needed whack-a-mole explained to them. The foam mallet is an invitation. The popping targets are irresistible. Kids start swinging before you finish the rules.

6. **FACE-TO-FACE RIVALRY** — After both rounds, the players KNOW each other's scores. The swapped roles mean they've experienced each other's strategy from both sides. "You were SO fast with the fake-outs!" "Yeah but you got me 14 times!" This creates post-game conversation, respect, and rematches. It's a social game, not a solo experience.

7. **INFINITELY REPLAYABLE** — Because the "AI" is human, no two rounds are the same. Different operators create completely different experiences. A kid could play 10 times and face 10 different mole patterns. The game literally never repeats.

8. **SPECTATOR GOLD** — The front-side view (kid frantically whacking) is hilarious. But the BEHIND-THE-BOARD view (kid popping plungers, grinning maniacally, timing their fake-outs) is equally entertaining. Two spectator vantage points, both worth watching.

## Vibe
Think: Japanese game show meets classic arcade meets backyard chaos. The board is a 4ft slab of comedy. Mole faces pop out of holes. A kid with a foam bat goes absolutely feral. Behind the board, their opponent is grinning and plotting the next fake-out. The BONK sound echoes across the carnival. The crowd is howling. Nobody can believe this low-tech masterpiece is the most fun game at the fair. Because it IS.
