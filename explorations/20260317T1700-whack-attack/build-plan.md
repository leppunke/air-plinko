# Whack Attack — Build Plan

## Concept
Manual two-player whack-a-mole. A 48"×36" board stands vertically with 12 holes. Player One hides behind the board and pops foam-topped plungers through the holes in random patterns. Player Two stands in front with a foam mallet and whacks the targets back through. Roles swap after each round. The other player IS the game engine — human-powered difficulty that automatically adapts to skill level.

## Modes
- **Head-to-Head (default):** 30 sec each role, swap. Most whacks wins
- **Endurance:** One whacker, one operator. How many whacks in 60 seconds?
- **Gauntlet:** Line of 5+ operators, each gets 15 seconds operating. One whacker tries to maintain a streak against increasingly devious opponents
- **Team Battle:** 2v2 — one whacker + one operator per team, simultaneous on two boards

## Architecture

### 1. Whack Board (The Main Event)

A 48"×36" sheet of ¾" plywood with 12 holes cut in a 4×3 grid.

- **Material:** ¾" plywood — thick enough to be sturdy, thin enough that foam plunger heads pop through cleanly
- **Holes:** 4" diameter, cut with a hole saw or jigsaw. Sanded smooth inside (no splinters for foam heads to catch on)
- **Hole layout:** 4 columns × 3 rows, evenly spaced:
  - First hole center: 7" from left, 7" from bottom
  - Horizontal spacing: 11.3" between centers
  - Vertical spacing: 11" between centers
  - This gives ~3" clearance from edges on all sides
- **Paint/theme:** Front face painted as a grassy field with mole hills. Holes are "mole holes" with painted dirt rings around them. Optional: googly eyes and mole face details around each hole. Back face left natural or painted a flat color
- **Hole labels:** Each hole gets a number (1–12) painted above it — helps the operator track which holes they're using

### 2. A-Frame Base

The board stands vertically, supported by an A-frame that prevents tipping.

- **Construction:** Two triangular side supports, each made from:
  - A 36" vertical 2×4 (attaches to board edge)
  - A 24" angled 2×4 (ground brace, extends back at ~30°)
  - Connected with a carriage bolt at the top and a cross brace at the bottom
- **Board attachment:** The board bolts to the two vertical 2×4s with carriage bolts + wing nuts (4 per side). Tool-free setup
- **Cross brace:** A horizontal 2×4 (48") connects the two A-frame bases at floor level for lateral stability
- **Feet:** Rubber caps on all ground-contact points. Optional: sandbag hooks on the rear braces for extra stability
- **Footprint:** The A-frame extends ~24" behind the board, giving the operator standing room
- **Board height:** Bottom of board sits ~12" off the ground (on the A-frame). This puts the center holes at ~30" — chest height for a 2nd grader, waist height for a 5th grader

### 3. Foam Plungers (The "Moles")

12 plungers, each a dowel with a foam ball head.

- **Dowel:** ½" wooden dowel, 18" long. Sanded smooth. Bottom 4" wrapped in grip tape or duct tape for comfortable handling
- **Foam head:** 3.5" diameter foam ball (craft foam sphere from any hobby store), impaled on the dowel end with hot glue reinforcement. The 3.5" head fits through the 4" hole with ¼" clearance — pops through cleanly, doesn't jam
- **Foam head decoration:** Painted as mole faces. Brown base, pink nose, googly eyes (hot-glued, secured with clear packing tape overlay for durability). OPTIONAL but HIGHLY RECOMMENDED — the googly eyes bouncing as plungers pop through holes is comedy gold
- **Handle end:** A small rubber cap or wooden ball on the bottom prevents the plunger from pushing all the way through the hole when operators get enthusiastic
- **Count:** Build 14 (12 + 2 spares). They're cheap and take 5 minutes each

### 4. Foam Mallet (The Weapon)

The whacker's tool — a foam bat that's satisfying to swing but can't damage anything.

- **Option A (pool noodle):** Cut a pool noodle to 18" length. Insert a ¾" dowel (24" long) into the center hole. The noodle extends 18" above the handle, creating a soft, wide striking surface. Duct tape the noodle to the dowel at the base. Total length: ~28"
- **Option B (foam bat):** Buy a foam baseball bat ($5 at any toy store). Done
- **Option C (custom):** Wrap a ¾" dowel (24") with pipe insulation foam (2" dia), duct tape to secure. Add a foam ball on the striking end for satisfying impact. Total length: ~24"
- **Recommendation:** Option A (pool noodle) — cheapest, easiest, most satisfying BONK sound, widely available
- **Count:** Build 2 (one in use, one spare)

### 5. Scoring System

- **Method:** The mole operator counts successful whacks. A "successful whack" = the mallet strikes the foam head and pushes the plunger back through the hole (or the operator feels the impact and withdraws). The operator keeps a running verbal count or uses a handheld tally counter ($3)
- **Verification:** The operator is the scorekeeper (honor system). For competitive rounds, a parent volunteer can stand to the side and verify by watching both the front and the peeking around
- **Display:** A whiteboard or chalkboard mounted on a nearby easel shows the current match score
- **Round structure:** 30-second rounds. Use a phone timer or kitchen timer with a loud beep

### 6. Operator Station (Behind the Board)

- **Standing area:** The operator stands behind the board in the A-frame space (~24" deep). They can reach all 12 holes by stepping side to side
- **Plunger rack:** A horizontal 2×4 rail mounted to the A-frame backs, at waist height, with 12 small hooks or clips. The operator hangs unused plungers here for quick access. In practice, operators carry 1-2 plungers at a time (one in each hand)
- **Sight gap:** The operator can peek over the top of the board (it's only 36" tall + 12" off ground = 48" total — shorter than most adults and taller 5th graders). This lets them see the whacker's position and strategically pop plungers in their blind spots
- **Feet visible:** The operator's feet are visible below the board (12" ground clearance). Expert whackers learn to read the operator's foot movement to predict which side the next mole will appear. This emergent gameplay is brilliant and unintentional

### 7. Safety Screen (Optional)

For younger kids who might swing the mallet through the hole and bonk the operator:
- **Construction:** Mount a sheet of ¼" hardware cloth (wire mesh) across the back of each hole, 2" behind the opening. The foam plungers can push through the mesh opening (it flexes), but a mallet cannot reach through
- **Alternative:** Trust the foam. The mallet head (pool noodle) is too wide to fit through a 4" hole. The dowel handle could poke through, but the mole heads are only exposed for 1–2 seconds. Risk is very low. **Recommendation:** Skip the mesh for simplicity, rely on the wide mallet head as natural protection

## Build Sequence

### Phase 1: Whack Board (Saturday Morning — ~2 hours)
1. Cut plywood to 48"×36" (or buy pre-cut)
2. Mark 12 hole positions in a 4×3 grid (measure and mark centers)
3. Cut 12 holes with 4" hole saw. If no hole saw: drill a pilot hole, use jigsaw to cut circles
4. Sand ALL hole edges smooth — inside and outside. These are the highest-friction surfaces in the game
5. Sand board faces and edges — no splinters anywhere
6. Base coat front face green (grass)
7. Paint brown dirt rings around each hole
8. Paint hole numbers (1–12) above each hole
9. Add mole hole details: little dirt mounds, grass tufts, worm tails
10. Optional: Paint the back face a flat color (brown, or paint it as underground tunnels — visible to the operator)

### Phase 2: A-Frame Base (Saturday Morning — ~1 hour, concurrent with paint drying)
1. Cut two 36" vertical 2×4s
2. Cut two 24" angled braces (cut one end at ~30° for flush floor contact)
3. Cut one 48" horizontal cross brace
4. Assemble two A-frame sides: bolt vertical + angled brace together at top
5. Attach board to verticals with carriage bolts + wing nuts (4 per side)
6. Attach cross brace between the two A-frames at floor level
7. Add rubber feet to all ground-contact points
8. Test stability — the board should stand firmly and not rock when struck with the mallet

### Phase 3: Foam Plungers (Saturday Afternoon — ~1 hour)
1. Cut 14 dowels to 18" lengths (12 + 2 spares)
2. Impale foam balls on one end of each dowel. Push in ~2". Hot glue to secure
3. Paint foam balls brown (mole base color). Let dry
4. Paint pink noses, dot eyes (or apply googly eyes + tape overlay)
5. Add grip tape to handle end (bottom 4")
6. Add rubber cap or wooden ball to handle end (prevents push-through)
7. Test each plunger in each hole — should pop through smoothly, foam head fully visible from front

### Phase 4: Mallet (10 minutes)
1. Cut pool noodle to 18"
2. Insert ¾" dowel through center of noodle
3. Duct tape noodle to dowel at base
4. Test: swing at a plunger popping through a hole. Should make a satisfying BONK. Plunger should push back through

### Phase 5: Testing & Tuning
1. Set up board on A-frame
2. One person operates plungers, another person whacks
3. Verify all 12 holes work — plungers pop through cleanly, mallet can reach all holes from the front
4. Check operator ergonomics — can they reach all 12 holes while standing behind the board?
5. Test pacing — operator should be able to pop 2 plungers simultaneously (one per hand)
6. Verify board stability — aggressive mallet swings shouldn't rock the frame
7. Time a 30-second round — how many pops can the operator do? (Target: 25–40 pops per round)
8. Measure fun — is the BONK sound satisfying? Are the googly eyes shaking? Are both players laughing?

## Safety Notes
- **Foam everything:** The mallet is foam. The plunger heads are foam. Nobody gets hurt
- Pool noodle mallet head is wider than the 4" holes — physically impossible to push the mallet through a hole to the operator
- Board is only 36" tall at its top edge (48" including ground clearance) — no climbing hazard
- A-frame is stable and weighted — mallet impacts don't tip it
- Plunger handle end caps prevent operators from accidentally pushing a bare dowel through
- The 12" ground clearance means operators' feet are visible — kids won't reach under the board (and if they do, they see feet, not machinery)
- Whiffle-grade safety: the most dangerous thing at this station is a kid tripping over their own feet while swinging
