# PINBALL PANIC

## The One-Liner
A giant walk-up pinball machine where kids ARE the flippers.

## Concept
Take the most addictive arcade game ever made. Make it 8 feet long. Replace the buttons with full-body paddle-swinging. Put it at a school carnival. Watch every parent in a 50-foot radius suddenly "need to check on their kid."

**PINBALL PANIC** is a supersized, walk-up pinball machine built from plywood, lumber, and 3D-printed parts. Two players stand in "flipper pits" — cutout stations at the bottom of a gently tilted 4ft × 8ft playfield. Each player grips a real handle attached to a hinged wooden paddle (the flipper). Balls launch from a spring-loaded plunger at the top, cascade down through a field of rubber-capped bumpers, spinning targets, and ramp lanes, and the players swing their flippers to keep balls alive and aim for scoring zones.

## Why Parents Can't Resist

1. **Nostalgia is a drug.** Every parent grew up pumping quarters into pinball machines. Seeing one at carnival scale triggers something primal.
2. **The skill gap is visible.** Parents watch a 2nd grader flailing at the flippers while balls drain and think: *"I could time that bumper combo perfectly."*
3. **The sounds.** Balls rattling off bumpers, the THWACK of a flipper connecting, the bell when you hit the jackpot target. It's a siren song.
4. **It's physical.** This isn't pressing a button — you're swinging a 2-foot paddle. Parents see the full-body motion and want IN.
5. **Spectacle.** A giant pinball machine is a visual magnet. It draws a crowd just by existing.

## Gameplay

### Setup
- 2 players per round (one per flipper)
- 5 balls per game
- 60-second timer (keeps the line moving)
- Cooperative: both flippers work together, combine score vs. target
- Competitive: each flipper has a "save zone" — if YOU miss, only YOU lose points

### How It Works
1. **LOAD:** Operator (or older kid volunteer) drops a ball into the launch chute at the top
2. **PLUNGE:** Player pulls back a spring-loaded plunger handle — ball rockets onto the playfield
3. **CHAOS:** Ball cascades down through bumpers, deflectors, and ramp lanes — bouncing, spinning, ringing bells
4. **FLIP:** When the ball reaches the bottom, players swing their flipper paddles to send it back up
5. **SCORE:** Hit lit-up targets for points. Nail the center JACKPOT ramp for 500 points and a ball return to the top
6. **DRAIN:** Miss with both flippers? Ball drops into the gutter. Next ball!

### Scoring Zones
| Zone | Points | Where |
|------|--------|-------|
| Pop bumpers (×6) | 10 each | Scattered across mid-field |
| Spinner gate | 25 (per spin) | Upper-left lane |
| Star targets (×3) | 50 each | Upper-right cluster |
| Loop ramp | 100 | Full left-side loop |
| JACKPOT ramp | 500 | Dead-center, hardest shot |

## The Vibe
Neon paint on black. UV reactive bumpers and lane markings. Battery-powered LED strip around the edges. Bells on the bumper posts. It looks like an 80s arcade exploded into a carnival booth. Kids line up because it looks rad. Parents hover because they *remember.*

## Key Design Decisions
- **Whiffle balls** (not heavy balls) — safe for all ages, won't damage the playfield
- **Gentle tilt (~10°)** — balls move slow enough for young kids to react, but fast enough that adults feel challenged
- **Oversized flippers (24" long)** — forgiving enough that even a kindergartner can make contact
- **Spring-loaded bumpers** — real bounce, real unpredictability, real chaos
- **Modular playfield** — bumpers, ramps, and targets screw into a grid of pre-drilled holes. Rearrange the layout year to year for a fresh experience
- **Two-person carry** — breaks into playfield + leg assembly. Two handles on each piece
