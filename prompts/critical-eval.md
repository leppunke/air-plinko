You just finished evaluating a carnival game concept you designed. Now put down the pom-poms.

Re-read the EVAL.md you wrote. Apply the following critical lens and edit it in place. Do not create a separate document. The evaluation should read as honest from the start — not as a hype piece with caveats bolted on.

## The Problem You're Solving

AI-generated evaluations of AI-generated concepts suffer from a predictable failure mode: self-congratulation. You designed the thing, then you graded the thing, and surprise — it's amazing. The scores cluster high, the language is breathless, every weakness has a ready-made "mitigation," and the verdict is always "BUILD IT." This is not evaluation. This is a sales pitch.

## Rules for the Critical Pass

### 1. Scores must differentiate
If every concept scores 8-10 on memorability, the rubric is broken. A 10 means "best-in-class across all carnival games that exist, not just the ones in this repo." A 7 means "good." A 5 means "average — on par with a typical school carnival booth." Use the full range. If a concept is basically a plinko board with a twist, it is not a 10/10 on memorability — plinko boards exist at every carnival already.

### 2. Kill the hype language
Remove or rewrite sentences that read like ad copy. "Instagram GOLD," "dopamine loop," "core memory machine," "this game defines the carnival" — these are feelings, not assessments. Replace them with specific, falsifiable observations. "The neon color scheme is visible from 30 feet" is an observation. "This is Instagram GOLD" is not.

### 3. Weaknesses must hurt
Every weakness section should contain at least one problem that could actually kill the concept or seriously degrade the experience. "The rubber bands might wear out (but they're $3 to replace!)" is not a real weakness. "Kindergartners can't physically operate the mechanism, excluding 20% of the target audience" is. If you can't find a painful weakness, you haven't thought hard enough.

### 4. Stop recommending everything
"Build it" is not a verdict — it's a reflex. A real evaluation ends with a clear-eyed trade-off statement. What does this concept sacrifice? What audience does it underserve? Under what conditions should you NOT build it? If you would genuinely build all 13 concepts, your evaluation process has failed.

### 5. The "would a stranger agree?" test
For each score, ask: if a parent volunteer who had never seen this concept read the build plan and watched a 30-second video of gameplay, would they independently arrive at this score? If your 10/10 memorability rating requires imagining an ideal scenario where everything works perfectly, it's not a 10.

### 6. Compare honestly
Stop grading each concept in isolation. If three concepts score 10/10 on memorability, at most one of them deserves it. The others are 8s that you talked yourself into. Anchor to the weakest concept in the set and score relative to it.

### 7. The "what actually happens" test
Carnival day reality: it's 90°F, the volunteer running the booth is a parent who arrived 10 minutes ago, setup took longer than planned, and the line is 15 kids deep. Score for THAT scenario, not the ideal one where everything goes right and the lighting is perfect.

## Idempotency Rule

Running this critical pass a second time should produce no meaningful changes. If it does, the first pass wasn't honest enough. The goal is not pessimism — it's accuracy. A score that survives two passes of scrutiny is a score you can trust.

## What the Output Looks Like

Edit the EVAL.md directly. Same structure, same heuristics. But the scores should use the full 1-10 range, the language should be specific rather than breathless, the weaknesses should sting, and the verdict should be a real recommendation — including "don't build this" if that's the honest answer.
